Design Constraint

This project was developed under a constrained design condition, similar to a game jam or an exam.

Two keywords were given at the start:
Museum
Scale shifting

These constraints defined both the environment and the core mechanic direction.

All design decisions had to follow these two requirements.

Design Thinking

When I first saw the keywords, my initial idea was a stealth game.

A museum naturally supports stealth. It has guards, patrol routes, and controlled space.

However, stealth is a very common direction. Many people would arrive at the same idea.

I needed a way to make the project stand out.

Instead of changing the gameplay type, I changed the player identity.

I asked a different question:
What if the player is not entering the museum, but already inside it?

This idea led to a clear direction.

The player becomes an artifact inside the museum.

Core Concept

The player is a museum artifact that can switch between two forms.

Teapot Form is small and safe. It is used for stealth and movement.

Human Form is larger and exposed. It is used for interaction and attack.

Both form follows a different set of rules.

The player must switch between these two forms to progress.

This creates a dual-system structure inside the same level.

World Setup

The story takes place in a museum where artifacts have developed consciousness.

This consciousness comes from human attention.

The more an object is seen and appreciated, the stronger it becomes.

These artifacts can create a human-like projection.

This form appears as a vague figure. It can be seen by people, but not detected by surveillance systems.

They only move at night, when the museum is empty.

During this time, they gather, interact, and pass time together.

The player is a teapot that was once displayed in the exhibition hall.

It was loved by visitors and gained consciousness.

Over time, it was forgotten and moved into a storage room.

As attention faded, its consciousness weakened.

At the start of the game, the player can no longer leave the teapot's position in Human Form.

It is close to losing consciousness completely.

One day, another teapot finds the player.

This teapot is still on display and wants to escape the museum.

It proposes a deal.

A projection will be left in the display case for four hours to avoid detection.

During this time, the player must escape the storage room, move through the museum, avoid guards, find keys, to unlock doors and reach the exhibition hall.

The goal is return to the light and restore existence.

Systems

The prototype is built around a small number of core systems.

Each system supports the transformation mechanic and stealth gameplay.

Level Structure

The level uses a linear progression structure.

The player starts in the storeroom and moves forward step by step. Progression is controlled by key items placed in a fixed order.

This structure was chosen to control pacing, teaching order, and player attention.

It ensures the player can experience the full loop within a short demo.

Progression System

Progression is driven by three key items:

  • Storeroom key
  • Corridor key
  • Exhibition hall ID card

Each item unlocks the next area. The player cannot skip steps.

The main question is not where to go, but how to safely obtain these items under pressure.

Transformation System

The player switches between Teapot Form and Human Form.

Teapot Form:

  • small size
  • cannot be hit by bullets
  • cannot attack
  • used for observation and movement

Human Form:

  • larger size
  • can perform mental attack
  • exposed to enemy fire
  • used to solve problems and obtain items

The system forces the player to choose between safety and action.

Enemy Response System

Enemies do not die. They are disabled.

Mental attack reduces enemy mental stability. When it reaches zero, the enemy enters a 30-second stunned state.

During this window:

  • the enemy stops moving
  • the enemy cannot attack
  • the player can approach and take items

The first time the player attacks, a second guard joins the patrol.

This increases pressure and changes the safe routes in the level.

Health and Failure Rules

Teapot Form and Human Form use separate health values.

If Human Form health reaches zero:

  • the player is forced back to Teapot Form
  • Human Form is permanently lost

If Teapot Form health reaches zero:

  • the game ends

Human Form is designed as high risk and high value.

It is required to progress, but cannot be used carelessly.

Stealth Pressure Design

The level combines several pressure sources:

  • enemy patrol routes
  • timing windows for safe movement
  • laser traversal in the later section

The player must:

  • observe before acting
  • choose when to attack
  • plan the next move before triggering higher difficulty

The goal is to create tension without removing player control.

Loop

observe space
        ↓
switch form
        ↓
create an opening
        ↓
collect progress item
        ↓
return to stealth
        ↓
advance to the next area

The goal was to make each step clear and repeatable.

The loop focuses on timing, positioning, and risk control.

Challenge and Solution

The main challenge was designing two player forms that feel different but still work in the same level.

If the difference is too small, the system becomes meaningless. If the difference is too large, the gameplay breaks.

The solution was to assign each form a clear role.

Teapot Form is safe but limited.
Human Form is powerful but risky.

Each form has different rules, interaction options, and survival conditions.

This creates a clear decision space for the player.

Another challenge was production scope.

In 12 days, it was not possible to build multiple systems at high quality.

The solution was to focus on one mechanic, one level, and one complete loop.

Other ideas were tested but removed to protect stability and completion.

Unimplemented Systems (Scope Limitation)

Some systems were designed but not included in the final build due to time constraints.

These systems were planned to expand player feedback and narrative outcome.

1. Score System

The player starts with a score of 100.

When the player takes a key, the score decreases.

After opening all required doors, the player can return the key to its original position.

If the key is returned, the score increases back.

This system was designed to encourage careful play and responsibility.

The ending would change based on the final score.

For example:
If the player does not return all keys, the next day newspaper would report that museum keys have disappeared.
If the player performs well, the result would appear as a normal night with no incident.

2. Surveillance System

In Teapot Form, the player must avoid security cameras.

If detected by cameras, the player loses score.

This system adds another layer of stealth pressure beyond guards.

It forces the player to consider positioning and movement more carefully.

3. Narrative Feedback (Newspaper Ending)

After completing the level, the player would see a newspaper the next day.

The content changes based on player performance.

Possible variations include:
A normal report with no unusual activity
A report about missing keys
A report suggesting supernatural events in the museum

This system connects player behavior to narrative outcome.

These systems were not implemented in the prototype in order to ensure a complete and stable playable experience within the 12-day timeframe.

However, they are fully designed and can be integrated into future iterations. The Goal was to connect player behavior with narrative outcome, not just success or failure.
