Game Design Case Study

Milk Man
Crushing

Problem Solving and Innovation

Party Game Top Down Arena Chaotic Multiplayer Slippery Movement Cow Chase PLO 2

Milk Man Crushing is a multiplayer party game built around chaotic movement, shared objectives, and player disruption. The project evolved through several rounds of testing, with each iteration focused on solving a different player experience problem.

1 Initial Prototype

The first playable version simplified the original concept into a functional prototype.

Players collected milk as a progress value. The win condition was reaching one hundred percent milk. Shooting was separated into another system with its own cooldown. Players used one trigger to collect milk and another trigger to shoot.

The game was presented as a top down 2D arena. Players were represented as simple colored shapes moving on a very slippery surface. Movement was intentionally unstable to create chaotic control and unpredictable interactions.

The cow moved autonomously and could push players away. There was no health system, no elimination, and no ghost mechanic. The game focused entirely on reaching the milk goal before others.

2 Iteration One

Problem Discovery

During the first round of playtesting, players consistently avoided using the shooting mechanic. Players focused only on collecting milk and ignored shooting even when it was available.

Cause Analysis

The shooting system did not provide enough value compared to its cost. Players needed to manage cooldowns and use an additional button, but the impact was not strong enough to justify the effort.

Design Decision

The design direction shifted toward increasing interaction and reducing friction. The shooting mechanic was redesigned to become a core interaction instead of an optional tool.

Implementation

A stun system was introduced. Players now accumulate stun when hit, and reaching a threshold temporarily disables movement. The shooting mechanic was changed to unlimited use.

Key outcome: players started using the interaction system instead of ignoring it.

3 Iteration Two

Problem Discovery

Some players found the movement system too difficult to control. The slippery movement created a skill gap between players, which reduced accessibility for casual players.

Cause Analysis

The game provided only one difficulty level and one dominant play pattern. Players with less control struggled to participate effectively.

Design Decision

The design direction shifted toward flexibility and player choice. The system needed to support different skill levels.

Implementation

A mode selection system was introduced. Three difficulty modes: Easy (reduced speed), Normal (original), Hard (click-to-collect, milk as ammo). Supports 2-6 players.

Key outcome: the project moved from one fixed play pattern to multiple player friendly modes.

4 Iteration Three

Problem Discovery

Players experienced long periods without control due to repeated stun effects. The game became less playable in larger groups.

Cause Analysis

The stun system did not limit stacking effects and was not balanced for higher player counts.

Design Decision

The system needed to maintain chaos while preserving player control.

Implementation

Stun accumulation was disabled during stun states. Stun duration reduced from five seconds to one second. Audio and music feedback were added.

Key outcome: the game kept its chaotic energy without locking players out of play.

5 Final Validation

After these adjustments, playtesting showed no major negative feedback.

Players remained active for most of the game. Interaction stayed frequent, but control was preserved. The game achieved a better balance between chaos and playability.

The final version preserved the party game energy while making the system clearer, fairer, and more responsive across different player counts.

6 Reflection

This project demonstrates how player behavior reveals problems that are not visible in design intentions.

The first iteration showed that unused mechanics indicate a mismatch between effort and reward. The second iteration showed that accessibility and depth must be balanced to support different players. The third iteration showed that systems must scale with player count.

Each design change was guided by direct observation of player experience rather than assumptions.

In future projects, systems will be tested earlier with a focus on player behavior, and mechanics will be evaluated based on how often and how naturally players choose to use them.
