WONDER
TALES 鬼神奇谭

A narrative RPG concept blending ancient Chinese folklore with Western cosmic horror

Players step into the role of Western scholars entering a mysterious version of ancient China - uncovering spirits, curses, rituals, and forbidden forces through choices, relationships, and collectible story cards.

Game Summary

Wonder Tales is a narrative-driven RPG concept that combines Chinese folklore traditions with Western cosmic horror. Players take the role of Western scholars entering a mysterious version of ancient China, where spirits, curses, and ritual practices hide something far older beneath the surface.

Through narrative choices, relationship changes, and collectible story cards, players gradually uncover secrets hidden inside a world that first looks peaceful - until its rules begin to fracture.

This page records not only what the game is, but also how the concept changed over time: the first version, what failed in paper prototype playtests, why I changed direction, and what still needs to be solved.

Core Statement

A solo-friendly narrative RPG built around a loop of choice → relationships → story cards → new scenes → journal updates, where the outsider perspective makes cultural discovery readable - and cosmic horror escalates without replacing the folklore foundation.

Narrative System Structure

The narrative system connects player decisions, relationships, and story progression so choices create both immediate emotional consequences and long-term story impact.

Narrative Loop

Player Choice → Relationship Change → Card Unlock → New Story Scene → Journal Entry Update → Branching Narrative Path

Step 1: What Kind of Game Do I Want to Make?

I want a game that is narrative-driven, full of meaningful choices, rich in character and relationship-building, and built around a card system that adds emotional and strategic depth.

At this stage, I did not start from visuals first. I started from production reality and my own strengths: if I have to build this mostly alone, the game loop must be writing-heavy, choice-driven, and systems-light enough to ship.

Step 2: Theme — When, Where, Who?

I want a haunting journey through ancient China, blending fear, the supernatural, and stories about love, betrayal, and friendship. Cultural traditions and philosophical ideas should shape gameplay and make it emotionally and intellectually engaging.

Why Horror and Chinese Themes

Horror games like Resident Evil and Silent Hill have loyal fanbases. Titles like Paper Bride and Detention show how Chinese folklore can stand out in a crowded market. Culturally specific stories attract global audiences curious about non-Western traditions.

Why Mystical and Religious Themes

These themes add depth to puzzles and mystery-solving. A semi-authentic religious system keeps players curious. Subtle storytelling can teach players about Chinese culture in a natural way.

Market Example

First Stage Playtest (TRPG Format)

Goal: Test narrative engagement, player choice, and mechanics through a mini TRPG campaign.

This was a paper prototype phase, not a content showcase. I mainly used it to test if players could understand the world quickly, role-play naturally, and stay engaged in branching scenes without heavy onboarding.

How It Worked

Players split into small groups with unique manuscripts, built custom characters, interacted with NPCs and maps under a time system, and reached different endings through choices.

What Worked

Players liked the creative scenarios.

What Did Not Work

Three issues appeared repeatedly: non-Chinese players needed too much background knowledge, purely Chinese modules felt monotonous over time, and custom player characters often felt like outsiders to the narrative logic.

Suggestions

Simplify cultural references, add more dynamic and thrilling elements, and better integrate player characters into the world.

Solution

I redesigned the protagonist as a Western outsider. This gave me a natural narrative reason to explain Chinese folklore in plain language and made player learning and character learning happen at the same pace.

New Core Statement

The protagonists are outsiders. They experience, learn, and explain ancient Chinese folklore in ways that are easy for non-native audiences to understand, lowering the cultural entry barrier.

Second Stage Research and Design

Adding Western Horror

This was not a random aesthetic mix. It was a response to playtest problems: I needed a structure that could keep Chinese folklore authentic while giving global players a familiar horror entry point.

Why Cthulhu Mythos

I chose Cthulhu Mythos because its investigator framework and cosmic dread can sit beside ritual-based Chinese horror without replacing it.

H. P. Lovecraft

Introduced ancient gods, cosmic horror, and forbidden knowledge through works including The Call of Cthulhu, At the Mountains of Madness, and The Shadow over Innsmouth.

COC TRPG Influence

Players become investigators facing supernatural threats, with themes of investigation, survival, sanity, and cosmic horror.

Miskatonic University

A fictional university in Arkham, Massachusetts, known for studying the occult, myths, and forbidden knowledge.

Final Game Setting

Time and Place

The game is set in 1797, when China was mostly closed to the outside world, blending history with fictional mysticism.

Main Characters

Four recent graduates of Miskatonic University, each with different backgrounds, gender, and specialties that affect gameplay.

Story Premise

In 1798, each protagonist enters China and finds chaos, madness, and ancient secrets beneath the appearance of peace.

Player Goal

Explore a semi-historical, semi-fictional China through one chosen character and navigate rituals, cosmic disturbances, and hidden dangers.

In short, the final direction keeps the Chinese core as the world foundation, uses the outsider perspective as the narrative bridge, and uses Western cosmic horror as pressure and escalation - not as the center.

Iteration Path and Next Problems

Path in One Line

Chinese folklore only (v1) → paper prototype feedback → outsider protagonist + East-West horror fusion (v2).

What Is Clear Now

The design intention is now consistent with production constraints: solo-friendly scope, choice-heavy narrative, and clear audience onboarding.

Open Problem 1

How to keep cultural explanations simple without flattening historical depth and local nuance.

Open Problem 2

How to balance relationship routes, card progression, and mystery pacing so replay value feels meaningful, not repetitive.

Open Problem 3

How to maintain horror tension while supporting multiple protagonists and route branches with reasonable solo production cost.

Next Step

Build a second-round vertical slice focused on one chapter, then validate readability, pacing, and branching clarity with cross-cultural testers.

试阅

December, 1797

This day I filed the necessary papers for my graduation from Miskatonic University.

January, 1798

The matter has been approved.

In this profession, to graduate is seldom a simple distinction; more often it is merely proof that one has survived what so many others did not.

When I informed my advisor of my intention to depart for China, he regarded me with an expression I found difficult to interpret—part approval, and part something darker which I could not readily name. He had always possessed a certain fondness for the unexplored corners of the world.

China, as it stands, has remained closed since the year of '57. Forty-one years of silence.

By all official accounts, it is the one place upon this earth where nothing extraordinary occurs.

That fact alone ought to have given me pause.

As I stepped into the carriage, he laid a firm hand upon my shoulder—so firm that it seemed less the gesture of a teacher dismissing a pupil than of a man offering farewell to one who may not return.

“Luck,” he said.

30 January, 1798

At last I have made landfall in China, having approached by the water route.

The officials at the port were plainly unprepared for my arrival.

Thus, after forty-one years, the wall which has sealed this land from the outside world has received its first small fracture.

Should this journal ever fall into other hands—whenever or wherever that may be—know that by the time you read these words, you too will stand upon the threshold of the same depths into which I have now ventured.

For that reason, I confess I welcome the thought of company.

Luck.
