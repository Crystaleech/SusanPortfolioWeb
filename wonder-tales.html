Project Detail / Wonder Tales

WONDER
TALES 鬼神奇谭

A 2D narrative-driven card horror game concept blending ancient Chinese folklore with Western cosmic horror

Players take the role of outsider investigators entering a mysterious version of ancient China, progressing through branching story events with narrative choices, card-based interactions, relationship shifts, and journal updates as they uncover spirits, rituals, curses, and hidden cosmic forces.

Early Design Question

This project started with a simple design question: can a card-based narrative structure support a horror experience that feels tense, readable, and emotionally engaging?

At the beginning, I was not starting from a full world setting yet. I was more interested in the relationship between cards, narrative, and horror. I wanted to know whether cards could do more than carry items or choices. I wanted them to help organize story information, control pacing, and make each decision feel heavier.

This led to the first direction of the project: a narrative horror game where players move through story events with choices and cards, while gradually uncovering danger through play.

Core Statement

To answer the design question, the core statement of the project became clear:

Wonder Tales explores how a narrative card game can present culturally specific horror in a way that stays readable to players, even if they are unfamiliar with the source culture.

To follow the core statement, the design then decided to focus on a structured investigation loop. Players do not learn the world through long explanation. Instead, they learn by making choices, unlocking narrative cards, updating relationships, and slowly understanding the logic behind the horror.

The goal is to let clarity, immersion, and dread grow together through play.

Game Pillars

Based on the core statement, I built four game pillars to guide the project:

Discovery Over Explanation

  • Players should learn the world through interaction and progression.
  • If the setting only works when someone explains it from outside the game, the design is not doing its job.

Horror Through Interpretation

  • Fear should come from understanding, not only from surprise.
  • The more players learn about the world, the more threatening that world should become.

Clarity Enables Immersion

  • Players should always understand who they are, what they can do, and what their short-term goal is.
  • Mystery should come from the world itself, not from unclear systems.

Structure Supports Freedom

  • Narrative freedom only works when the player has a clear structure to act within.
  • Choices, cards, and progression need to form a loop that players can trust and understand.

Experience Design Priorities

After defining the game pillars, I translated them into concrete experience design priorities. These priorities helped me decide what the player should feel first, what should stay clear, and what should change over time.

Accessible Cultural Discovery

Players should learn unfamiliar folklore through play, perspective, and progression, not through long outside explanation.

Controlled Horror Escalation

The experience should begin with curiosity, move into tension, and slowly grow into dread.

Clear Investigative Role

At every stage, players should understand who they are, what they know, and what kind of action the game is asking them to take.

Meaningful Narrative Consequences

Choices should not only change dialogue. They should also affect relationships, information access, and the direction of the investigation.

Theme and World Direction

After defining the core statement, game pillars, and experience priorities, I began shaping the game’s theme and world direction.

At this stage, I decided to ground the project in Chinese folk horror. I chose this direction because it offers a strong mix of ritual, taboo, symbolic objects, and socially rooted supernatural belief. These qualities fit the kind of horror I wanted to build: fear that grows through interpretation, uncertainty, and gradual understanding.

This choice also created a new design challenge. Because many players may not already know this cultural context, the world needed to stay specific without becoming hard to follow. So the setting direction became clear: build a horror world with strong cultural identity, but present it through a structure that players can enter step by step.

First Stage Research: Chinese Folklore

Once the theme direction was clear, I began the first stage of research. The goal of this stage was to find folklore elements that could become playable sources of mystery, tension, and horror.

Paper Dolls

Paper dolls suggest a blurry line between object and spirit. They are useful for horror because they make symbolic objects feel alive and unsettling.

Ghost Marriage

Ghost marriage introduces ritual, obligation, death, and unfinished relationships. It creates emotional horror rooted in custom and social meaning.

Puppetry

Puppetry brings ideas of control, imitation, and unseen manipulation. It is especially useful for a game about agency, misdirection, and fear of hidden forces.

Witchcraft

Witchcraft creates overlap between healing, revenge, protection, and danger. It allows ritual behavior to become both worldbuilding and gameplay logic.

This research gave the project a stronger cultural base and helped define what kinds of horror the game could use.

Moodboard and Atmosphere

After the first research stage, I used moodboards to define the game’s emotional and visual direction.

The atmosphere I wanted was something eerie, ancient, ritualistic, and quietly oppressive. The emotional flow needed to start with curiosity, then move into unease, and only later open into deeper horror.

At this stage, the moodboards helped me make the project more concrete. They turned abstract research into a clearer visual tone that could support future design and prototyping.

Initial Design Direction

With the research and moodboards in place, I formed the project’s first clear design direction for paper prototype testing.

In this version, players entered the world through self-created identities. The game used a multiplayer TRPG-style format, where live narration, group roleplay, and card-supported story events worked together to create immersion.

At the time, this approach made sense because it seemed flexible and atmospheric. It allowed players to react freely, make narrative choices, and experience the setting in a social way.

However, this version also carried a hidden risk: it depended heavily on player improvisation, facilitator explanation, and prior understanding of the setting. That risk became much more visible during playtesting.

First Stage Playtest (TRPG Format)

To test the first design direction, I ran a paper prototype in a live-narrated TRPG format.

In this playtest, I acted as the facilitator. I used music, sound, and written scenes to guide players through the story. Players created their own identities, entered the setting as a group, responded through roleplay and choices, and used cards as part of narrative progression.

The results were mixed. The atmosphere worked well, and many players were curious about the setting. The format created strong moments of immersion, especially during live narration.

However, three major problems appeared:

Non-Chinese players needed frequent explanation to understand folklore references.

Multiplayer pacing made attention uneven across the group.

Self-created characters often felt disconnected from the story’s internal logic.

This led to an important design insight: the problem was not lack of interest. The problem was that the experience depended too much on explanation, improvisation, and facilitation.

With the playtest results , I decided the next version needed a clearer player role, a more controlled narrative structure, and a format that could work naturally to explain concept inside game role without constant outside guidance.

Second Stage Research and Design

After the first playtest, I moved into a second stage of research and redesign.

The goal of this stage was to create a structure that could keep the cultural core of the project, while making the player’s role easier to understand and the story easier to follow for everyone, no matter cultural background.

With this goal in mind, a new question came up: how can we explain unfamiliar cultural concepts without breaking immersion?

In the first version, players often needed direct explanation to understand folklore elements. Every time the game paused to explain a term, the sense of immersion dropped.

So instead of trying to remove explanation, I started thinking about how to hide it inside the experience.

Then an idea came up: what if the player is not a local person, but an outsider?

This led to introducing Western investigator characters and a Western knowledge framework. Since the protagonists are not familiar with the local culture, it feels natural for them to question what they see, take notes, and try to understand unfamiliar terms.

As a result:

Self-explanation becomes part of character behavior

Journal updates and notes feel like investigation, not UI

Re-reading information supports discovery instead of repetition

This way, explanation no longer interrupts the experience. It becomes part of the story, the environment, and the player’s role.

At the same time, I realized I already had a useful reference from the way I tested the first version.

The project started as a TRPG-style prototype, and besides Dungeons & Dragons, another well-known TRPG system is Call of Cthulhu (COC, full name: Call of Cthulhu Role-Playing Game).

So instead of building everything from scratch, I decided to borrow part of its structure. I set the protagonists as investigators connected to Miskatonic University, which naturally supports note-taking, research, and investigation as core behaviors.

This choice made the player role much clearer. It also gave a strong narrative reason for why characters would document what they see, question unfamiliar events, and gradually uncover hidden truths.

The main references in this stage were:

Cthulhu Mythos

A strong model for mystery, gradual revelation, and hidden forces beyond normal understanding.

Call of Cthulhu TRPG

It clarified the structure of investigation and helped define what the player actually does in the story.

Miskatonic University

It provided a clear narrative anchor, explaining why the protagonists travel, investigate, and confront supernatural events.

Visual Language Strategy

At the same time, I also started thinking more clearly about visual storytelling.

Since this is a 2D narrative game, I explored using color contrast to separate the outsider world from the local supernatural world. This became a way to show the player’s gradual involvement with the setting, moving from observation to deeper entanglement.

Character Overview

As part of the second-stage redesign, I replaced self-created player characters with defined outsider protagonists.

All four protagonists are connected to Miskatonic University, but each one enters China from a different background, with different expertise, emotional tone, and investigative strengths. This change gave the story stronger internal logic and made the player role much easier to understand.

It also created new design value: clearer onboarding, more controlled narrative framing, route variation, and replayability through different perspectives.

In this version of the project, the characters are not just story decoration. They are part of the game structure itself.

Second Stage Playtest repot

The second-stage playtest was used to check whether the revised structure actually solved the main problems from the first version.

The results were much stronger. Players understood the investigator role earlier, followed the narrative more easily, and needed less outside explanation to stay oriented. The story felt more readable, while the atmosphere and cultural tone were still preserved.

This confirmed that the redesign was moving in the right direction. The outsider protagonist structure, clearer narrative framing, and more controlled progression all helped the experience become more stable and easier to understand.

At the same time, the test also showed that future work is still needed in pacing, replay value, and how information is revealed across routes.

Final Game Setting (For Now）

After the second-stage redesign and testing, the project arrived at its current version.

Time and Place

The game is set in the late eighteenth century, in a version of China shaped by historical isolation, folklore logic, and fictional supernatural disturbance.

Main Characters

Players take the role of foreign investigators connected to Miskatonic University. Each protagonist brings a different perspective, background, and route into the story.

Story Premise

What first appears to be a culturally unfamiliar but fascinating world slowly reveals deeper supernatural instability and hidden cosmic pressure.

Player Goal

Players investigate events, interpret rituals and local beliefs, make narrative choices, use cards, and progress through branching storylines in order to survive and understand the truth.

In this current version, Chinese folklore remains the world foundation, while cosmic horror works as a pressure layer that deepens dread and supports the investigation structure.

Iteration Path and Next Problems

The overall iteration path of the project is now clear:

Version 1 was a folklore-focused multiplayer paper TRPG with self-created identities.

Version 2 shifted toward defined outsider investigators, clearer narrative structure, East-West horror integration, and a more solo-friendly direction.

This change made the project more readable, more scalable, and closer to the intended player experience.

However, several design problems still remain:

Open Problem 1

How can the game deliver cultural information clearly without breaking discovery and immersion?

Open Problem 2

How can relationship progression, card unlock pacing, and mystery escalation stay meaningful across multiple playthroughs?

Open Problem 3

How can a digital version preserve horror intensity without relying on live narration and facilitator performance?

Next Step

The next step is to build a focused vertical slice around one chapter and test whether the revised structure can support clarity, immersion, and branching progression on its own.

My Role

Game Designer · Narrative Designer · System Designer

I designed the core concept, narrative structure, branching routes, player role framing, card-based progression, and relationship logic for the project.

I also planned and ran the paper prototype playtests, observed where players became confused or disengaged, and used that feedback to restructure the project from a freeform multiplayer prototype into a clearer and more scalable narrative game direction.

Game Overview

Wonder Tales is a 2D narrative-driven card horror game that combines Chinese folklore with Western cosmic horror. The player takes the role of an outsider investigator entering an unfamiliar world. Through narrative events, skill-based decisions, card interactions, and journal records, the player uncovers rituals, relationships, and hidden supernatural systems.

The game focuses on gradual understanding. The player is not given full explanations. Instead, knowledge is built through interaction, observation, and repeated engagement with the system.

The core experience moves from curiosity to uncertainty and then to dread. The player slowly realizes the world follows a logic that is different from their own, and that understanding this logic is necessary to survive.

The game is designed as a single-player experience for PC.

Core Fantasy

The player is an outsider investigator who does not fully understand the world they have entered. They must observe, interpret, and record information in order to make decisions. The player is not powerful. Their strength comes from knowledge, preparation, and interpretation.

Core Loop

The player enters a story scene and reads the situation. The player selects a skill to respond to the situation. A dice roll determines the outcome based on the selected skill. The result updates multiple systems, including relationships, cards, and journal entries. New information is unlocked, and the next scene becomes available. This loop repeats throughout the chapter.

Each loop reinforces learning. The player understands more about the world through the consequences of their actions.

Update Systems: Journal / Trust / Cards

Player Role and Perspective

The player controls a Western investigator connected to Miskatonic University. The character is not familiar with local customs, rituals, or beliefs.

This role is designed to solve the problem of explanation. Since the character is an outsider, asking questions, taking notes, and misunderstanding events are natural behaviors. This allows explanation to exist inside the narrative instead of interrupting it.

The player’s knowledge is limited and grows over time. The player must rely on recorded information, previous events, and interpretation.

Game Structure

The game is divided into chapters. Each chapter features one main companion character and a contained investigation. A chapter includes multiple narrative nodes, branching paths, key events, and a final deduction phase.

Each chapter takes approximately forty-five to seventy-five minutes to complete. The full game is expected to last four to six hours depending on player choices and replay.

Chapter Structure Diagram
Start→ Branching Nodes→ Key Event→ More Nodes→ Deduction Phase→ Ending

Skill System

The game uses a simplified skill system inspired by Call of Cthulhu. Skills represent different ways the player can interact with the world.

The final skill set includes Observe, Listen, Search, Insight, Occult, History, Medicine, Language, Persuade, Charm, Intimidate, Stealth, Dexterity, and Willpower.

Each narrative node presents a situation. The player selects one skill to respond. The player cannot retry the same node. Failure is permanent and becomes part of the experience.

Skills are not just mechanical tools. They define how the player approaches problems and shape the narrative outcome.

Dice System

The game uses a dice-based resolution system.

The player rolls a number between one and one hundred. The result is compared to the selected skill value.

If the result is less than or equal to the skill value, the action succeeds. If the result is greater than the skill value, the action fails.

If the result is less than or equal to half of the skill value, the action is a strong success. If the result is very high, it can trigger a critical failure.

This system allows different levels of outcomes and supports varied narrative responses.

Card System

The game uses cards to represent characters, items, and knowledge.

Character cards represent key figures in the story. Each chapter includes one main companion character who stays with the player. This character also serves as the center of the relationship system.

Item cards represent physical objects. They can be equipped to provide bonuses or enable specific interactions.

Memory cards represent knowledge, clues, and past experiences. They can also be equipped and often unlock new choices or improve understanding of events.

Cards can modify skill values, provide passive effects, or create conditional bonuses. Players can equip cards to themselves and to the companion character. The card system replaces traditional inventory and knowledge tracking systems.

Relationship System

Each companion character has a trust value ranging from zero to one hundred.

Trust directly affects gameplay. High trust makes skill checks easier. Low trust makes them harder.

Trust also affects narrative access. High trust can unlock new dialogue, additional information, and alternative paths. Low trust can limit information and reduce available options.

This system connects narrative decisions to mechanical outcomes.

Journal System

The journal is an automatic recording system. It stores events, clues, and outcomes.

The journal records information based on what the character experiences, not the objective truth.

If a player fails a skill check, the journal records the failed perception. For example, failing a listening check results in an entry stating that nothing was heard.

The journal can be reviewed at any time. It supports investigation and helps the player track information across the chapter.

Missing key information can lead to incorrect conclusions later in the game.

Deduction System

At the end of each chapter, the player enters a deduction phase.

The player reviews journal entries and selects key information to form a conclusion.

If the player has enough correct information, they reach a successful outcome. If important information is missing or misinterpreted, the player reaches a negative outcome.

This system gives weight to earlier decisions and reinforces the importance of careful observation.

Narrative Design

The narrative is built around gradual revelation. Information is not given all at once. The player must connect fragments over time.

Horror is created through interpretation, not sudden shock. The more the player understands, the more threatening the world becomes.

Choices are meaningful. Each decision affects relationships, information access, and progression.

The story is structured to support replay. Different skills, card setups, and relationship levels lead to different outcomes.

Visual Direction

The game uses color contrast as a storytelling tool.

The outsider world is presented with more natural and stable colors. The supernatural world is represented with high contrast and controlled color accents.

As the player progresses, elements of the supernatural world begin to appear more frequently. This visual shift reflects the player’s deeper involvement with the setting.

The visual design supports the transition from observation to immersion.

Technical Requirements

The game must support branching narrative structures. Player choices and outcomes must be recorded and affect future events.

The system must track skill values, modifiers, and dice results. Card effects must be applied dynamically.

The journal system must record and display entries based on player actions.

The relationship system must update in real time and influence gameplay outcomes.

The system must support multiple endings based on player performance in the deduction phase.

MVP Scope

The minimum version of the game includes one complete chapter.

This chapter includes one companion character, a limited set of skills, a functional card system, a working journal system, and a complete deduction phase.

The goal of the MVP is to test whether the core loop, skill system, and narrative structure can work together without external explanation.
