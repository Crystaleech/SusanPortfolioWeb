Project Detail / Wonder Tales

WONDER
TALES 鬼神奇谭

A 2D narrative-driven card horror game concept blending ancient Chinese folklore with Western cosmic horror

Players take the role of outsider investigators entering a mysterious version of ancient China, progressing through branching story events with narrative choices, card-based interactions, relationship shifts, and journal updates as they uncover spirits, rituals, curses, and hidden cosmic forces.

Game Summary

Wonder Tales is a 2D narrative-driven card horror game concept that combines Chinese folklore traditions with Western cosmic horror. Players move through story scenes by making choices, unlocking narrative cards, and reshaping relationships while gradually uncovering a hidden layer of supernatural and cosmic threat.

The project began as a paper prototype influenced by TRPG and Call of Cthulhu-style live narration. In its first form, players built their own characters, responded to narrated scenarios, and advanced through the story using cards and choices in a group roleplay format.

Playtesting revealed a central design challenge: the setting generated strong curiosity, but many players needed too much explanation to stay oriented. The project then shifted toward a clearer narrative framework that could preserve atmosphere, cultural specificity, and player agency without depending so heavily on live facilitation.

Core Statement

This project explores how a narrative card game can support culturally specific horror while remaining readable to players unfamiliar with its source traditions. The design centers on a structured investigation loop in which players interpret events, unlock narrative cards, and build understanding through play, allowing clarity, immersion, and escalating dread to develop together.

Player Experience Goal

Create a horror experience that begins with curiosity, develops into uncertainty, and gradually escalates into dread as players uncover the logic behind an unfamiliar cultural and supernatural system.

Target Audience

Players who enjoy narrative-driven games, horror storytelling, and mystery investigation, including players with little prior knowledge of Chinese folklore.

Core Gameplay

A 2D narrative-driven card game where players move through branching story scenes by making choices, using narrative cards, and uncovering folklore and cosmic mysteries through a defined investigator role.

Narrative Loop

Choice → Relationship Shift → Narrative Card Unlock → New Story Scene → Journal Update → Branching Investigation Path

Narrative System Structure

The system connects choices, relationship shifts, card unlocks, and journal updates so that each decision produces both immediate emotional feedback and longer-term narrative consequences.

Actual Playtest Experience

The first paper playtest produced a mixed but valuable response. Players reacted strongly to the atmosphere, music, and live narration, but their overall experience was uneven. Some felt immersed and excited by the setting, while others were confused by unfamiliar folklore terms, uncertain about their role, or only followed the story passively.

Game Pillars

Discovery Over Explanation

  • Players should learn the world through interaction and progression, not through direct exposition.
  • If information must be explained outside gameplay, the design has failed.

Horror Through Interpretation

  • Fear should emerge from the player’s understanding of events, not just from visual or scripted shocks.
  • The more the player understands, the more threatening the world becomes.

Clarity Enables Immersion

  • Players must always understand their role, available actions, and immediate goals.
  • Confusion should come from the mystery, not from unclear systems or identity.

Structure Supports Freedom

  • Narrative freedom is meaningful only when supported by a clear underlying system.
  • Choices, cards, and progression must form a readable loop that players can rely on.

Early Design Question

The project began with a design question rather than a fixed story pitch: can a card-based narrative structure support a horror experience that feels both culturally specific and mechanically readable?

Card systems are strong at organizing information, gating progression, and making narrative choices feel tangible. Horror, by contrast, depends heavily on pacing, uncertainty, and emotional control. The first design challenge was to determine whether these two qualities could reinforce each other instead of competing with one another.

This led to the initial concept of a narrative card horror game in which players would move through story events by making choices, interpreting danger, and using cards as both narrative carriers and gameplay tools.

Choice as Tension

Narrative choice was treated as a source of tension, not just branching content. Decisions needed to feel risky, readable, and emotionally loaded.

Cards as Readable Information

Cards were selected as a structural tool because they can carry items, knowledge, relationships, and story fragments in a format players can quickly understand.

Narrative as Progression

Narrative functions as the main progression system through which new information and new actions become available.

Cultural Specificity as Strength

Chinese folklore serves as the core material through which mystery, fear, ritual, and interpretation emerge.

Theme and World Direction

The thematic direction focused on Chinese folk horror because it offers strong ritual logic, symbolic objects, and socially embedded supernatural beliefs. These elements support a form of horror built on interpretation, taboo, and shifting understanding rather than simple surprise.

The world design also needed to remain legible to players who were not already familiar with these traditions. This made theme selection part of the onboarding problem: the setting had to preserve cultural specificity while still giving players enough structure to read events as they unfolded.

Why Folk Horror

Folk horror supports fear through belief systems, ritual behavior, taboo, and interpretation. This makes it suitable for a game where knowledge itself changes how events are understood.

Why Chinese Folklore

Chinese folklore provides a rich structure of ghosts, funerary customs, ritual objects, and supernatural practices that can shape both narrative events and player expectations.

Design Implication

Because these themes are culturally dense, the design needed a framework that could gradually teach players how to read the world without interrupting the experience.

First Stage Research: Chinese Folklore

The first research phase focused on identifying folklore elements that could function as setting details and as playable sources of mystery, tension, and narrative interpretation.

Paper Dolls

Used in funerary and ritual contexts, paper dolls suggest the transfer of meaning and spirit into symbolic objects. They are useful for horror because they blur the boundary between representation and life.

Ghost Marriage

Ghost marriage introduces themes of obligation, death, social ritual, and unfinished relationships. It provides a strong foundation for emotionally charged horror rooted in custom rather than shock alone.

Puppetry

Puppetry introduces control, performance, imitation, and the fear of being manipulated by unseen forces. It is especially useful for a game interested in agency and misdirection.

Witchcraft

Witchcraft creates a space where healing, revenge, protection, and danger overlap. It allows ritual behavior to function as both narrative worldbuilding and possible gameplay logic.

Moodboard and Atmosphere

Moodboards were used to define the intended emotional tone: eerie, ancient, ritualistic, and quietly oppressive rather than purely violent. The visual goal was to support curiosity first, then unease, and only later open horror.

Initial Design Direction

Player Role Assumption

The first version assumed that players could enter the world through self-created identities and interpret the setting through roleplay freedom.

Format Assumption

The first prototype assumed that multiplayer TRPG-style interaction would strengthen immersion by making the experience more social and reactive.

Hidden Risk

This version depended heavily on player improvisation, facilitator explanation, and prior cultural understanding, which later became the central design problem.

First Stage Playtest (TRPG Format)

This first prototype was a paper playtest presented as a live-narrated TRPG experience. I acted as the facilitator, using music, sound, and written scenes to guide players through a horror story. Players created their own identities, entered the world in a group format, responded to events through roleplay and choices, and used cards as part of moment-to-moment narrative progression.

Prototype Format

Multiplayer paper prototype inspired by TRPG and Call of Cthulhu-style play. The experience relied on live narration, player-created identities, group interaction, and card-supported story progression.

Intended Experience

The design aimed to give players a sense of narrative freedom, strong immersion, and the thrill of entering a culturally unfamiliar horror world through active roleplay and group decision-making.

Actual Experience

The atmosphere worked well, especially with music and performed narration, but the overall experience was inconsistent. Some players were deeply engaged, while others struggled to follow the cultural references or understand what they were supposed to do.

Problems Identified

Three issues appeared repeatedly: non-Chinese players needed frequent explanation of folklore terms, multiplayer pacing distributed attention unevenly across the group, and self-created characters often felt disconnected from the story’s internal logic.

Design Insight

The main issue was not player interest in the setting. Cross-cultural players were curious about the world, but the experience depended too heavily on prior knowledge, improvisation, and facilitator explanation. That dependency interrupted immersion and made the format difficult to scale into a clearer game structure.

Resulting Change

The next iteration moved away from a freeform player-created group structure and toward a more controlled narrative framework with defined outsider protagonists, a clearer investigator identity, stronger in-world explanation tools, and a more structured single-player-compatible story loop.

Second Stage Research and Design

Adding Western Horror

Western cosmic horror was added to support the revised structure of the game. It provided a more familiar investigative framework for players, while Chinese folklore remained the cultural foundation of the world.

Why Cthulhu Mythos

Cthulhu Mythos provided an investigator structure, encounter logic, and gradual revelation model that fit a mystery-driven horror game and helped define what the player can actually do inside the story.

H. P. Lovecraft

Lovecraft’s work was relevant here mainly as a model for revealing unknown forces through documents, interpretation, and gradual discovery rather than direct spectacle.

COC TRPG Influence

Call of Cthulhu influenced both tone and structure. It clarified the player role as an investigator and gave narrative choices a stronger systemic frame.

Miskatonic University

Miskatonic University anchors the protagonists in a recognizable academic and investigative tradition. It explains why these characters travel, what kinds of knowledge they carry, and why they are capable of confronting supernatural events.

Visual Language Strategy

Because the game is designed as a 2D narrative experience, it cannot rely on realistic environmental storytelling in the same way as 3D horror games. This constraint led to a visual design decision: using color as a primary storytelling system.

The "outsider world" is presented with richer and more saturated colors, while the local supernatural world is represented through high-contrast black, white, and controlled red accents. This creates an immediate visual distinction between familiarity and cultural unknown.

As the player progresses and becomes more involved with the local world, the color boundary begins to shift. Elements from the supernatural layer gradually appear within the player’s visual space, suggesting deeper immersion and loss of distance.

This system is currently conceptual and has not yet been implemented, but it reflects a direct translation from research constraints into a planned player experience: moving from observation to entanglement through visual transformation.

Character Overview

All four protagonists are foreign students or academic figures connected to Miskatonic University. They enter China from different professional backgrounds, with different areas of expertise, narrative starting points, and investigative strengths. These differences support route variation, selective information access, and distinct emotional readings of the same larger mystery. In design terms, the multiple protagonists help structure replayability and allow the same world to be understood through different interpretive lenses.

Final Game Setting

Time and Place

The game is set in the late eighteenth century, in a version of China shaped by historical isolation, folklore logic, and fictional supernatural disturbance.

Main Characters

The playable protagonists are foreign investigators connected to Miskatonic University. Their backgrounds, expertise, and starting situations shape perspective, interpretation, and route-specific access to information.

Story Premise

Each protagonist enters an unfamiliar world that first appears culturally rich and mysterious, but gradually reveals deeper supernatural instability and hidden cosmic pressure.

Player Goal

Players investigate events, interpret unfamiliar ritual and social systems, and use narrative choices and cards to survive, understand, and progress through branching storylines.

The final direction keeps Chinese folklore as the world foundation, uses the outsider perspective as the narrative bridge, and uses cosmic horror as a pressure system that intensifies interpretation and dread rather than replacing the cultural core.

Iteration Path and Next Problems

Path in One Line

Version 1: folklore-focused multiplayer paper TRPG with self-created player identities → first playtest feedback → Version 2: defined outsider investigators, East-West horror integration, clearer card-based narrative structure, and a more solo-friendly direction.

What Is Clear Now

The revised concept aligns more closely with player onboarding needs, narrative clarity, and production reality. The design is now more readable, more scalable, and more consistent with the intended emotional experience.

Open Problem 1

How to deliver cultural information clearly without turning discovery into exposition.

Open Problem 2

How to balance relationship progression, card unlock pacing, and mystery escalation so replayability feels meaningful rather than repetitive.

Open Problem 3

How to preserve the emotional intensity of performed horror in a future digital version that cannot rely on a live facilitator.

Next Step

The next step is to build a focused vertical slice around one chapter and test whether the revised system can preserve immersion, communicate player role clearly, and support branching narrative progression without live explanation.

试阅

December, 1797

This day I filed the necessary papers for my graduation from Miskatonic University.

January, 1798

The matter has been approved.

In this profession, to graduate is seldom a simple distinction; more often it is merely proof that one has survived what so many others did not.

When I informed my advisor of my intention to depart for China, he regarded me with an expression I found difficult to interpret—part approval, and part something darker which I could not readily name. He had always possessed a certain fondness for the unexplored corners of the world.

China, as it stands, has remained closed since the year of '57. Forty-one years of silence.

By all official accounts, it is the one place upon this earth where nothing extraordinary occurs.

That fact alone ought to have given me pause.

As I stepped into the carriage, he laid a firm hand upon my shoulder—so firm that it seemed less the gesture of a teacher dismissing a pupil than of a man offering farewell to one who may not return.

“Luck,” he said.

30 January, 1798

At last I have made landfall in China, having approached by the water route.

The officials at the port were plainly unprepared for my arrival.

Thus, after forty-one years, the wall which has sealed this land from the outside world has received its first small fracture.

Should this journal ever fall into other hands—whenever or wherever that may be—know that by the time you read these words, you too will stand upon the threshold of the same depths into which I have now ventured.

For that reason, I confess I welcome the thought of company.

Luck.
